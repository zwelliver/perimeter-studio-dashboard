Priority Scoring & Capacity Allocation System
for Video Projects

Internal Reference Document — Updated February 2026

Introduction

We aim to steward the Studio and its production resources to align with our church leadership’s goals while efficiently and effectively meeting project needs. Every video follows a production pipeline—pre-production (~25%), production (~25%), and post-production (~50%)—each demanding personnel, time, and material costs.

This Priority Scoring & Capacity Allocation System is an automated tool that guides resource allocation and prioritization, supporting decision-making without being the sole decision-maker. All scoring is performed by AI (Grok) based on objective project analysis, with built-in safeguards against manipulation.

How the System Works

When a video project is submitted or created in Asana, the system automatically:

  1. Scores the project — AI analyzes the task name, notes, and content summary to assign a Priority Score, Complexity Rating, Category, and Type
  2. Calculates allocation — A formula converts scores into a weekly capacity percentage per team member
  3. Tracks capacity — The dashboard shows real-time team utilization, ensuring workload stays within sustainable limits
  4. Monitors delivery — On-time rates, variance, and at-risk projects are tracked continuously

The system runs every 15 minutes, keeping all scores, allocations, and dashboards current.

Priority Score Calculation

The priority score is calculated by adding the scores of three key factors: Purpose, Audience, and Scope. Each factor is rated on a scale from 1 to 4, resulting in a priority score ranging from 3 to 12. Higher scores indicate higher priority projects.

Formula:

Priority Score = Purpose + Audience + Scope

Factors for Priority Score

1. Purpose

Definition: The “why” behind the video—its alignment with the church’s mission and goals.

Scale:

  • 4: Equipping — Discipleship content for our church, relevant to the broader Church, or tied to Senior Pastor Jeff Norris. Examples: “Digging Deeper,” teaching series (e.g., “God Owns It All”), Strategic Partnerships (RightNow Media, Ron Blue, TGC), Jeff Norris sermon intros or content, Life on Life Missional Discipleship (LOL) videos.
  • 3: Enriching — “Inviting, Inspiring” stories and opportunities specific to Perimeter, especially seasonal (Holy Week, Easter, Christmas, Advent). Examples: Weekly Opportunity Video (WOV), member testimonies, campaign updates (Forward, Frontier), music videos, seasonal promos, Belong ministry videos.
  • 2: Ministry Support — Initiated by a formal Perimeter ministry to directly support it. Examples: event captures, recaps, highlights, promos, social media reels, ministry profiles for KidsRock, KidsQuest, GloryKids, KidsThrive, Rush, Camp All-American (CAA), Perimeter School, Men’s, Women’s, Young Adults.
  • 1: Partner Support — Requested by a partner or informal ministry to support them. Examples: church plant, local non-profit, Perimeter Village, global partner/ministry.

2. Audience

Definition: The “who” the video is intended to reach.

Scale:

  • 4: Church-wide — Relevant to most/all our congregation, often played Sundays, or tied to Senior Pastor Jeff Norris, Outdoor Events (OME), or Belong ministry. Examples: major updates, teaching series, Jeff Norris content, OME promos, Belong initiatives.
  • 3: Community — Represents Perimeter to a broader, external audience. Examples: “Plan Your Visit” for newcomers, public-facing promos.
  • 2: People Groups — Relevant to key demographics (e.g., men, women, marrieds, youth, students, children, camp families). Examples: videos for KidsRock, KidsQuest, GloryKids, KidsThrive, Rush, Camp All-American (CAA), Perimeter School, Men’s, Women’s, Young Adults.
  • 1: Ministry Groups — Relevant to specific groups, including partners (e.g., elders, camp staff, Perimeter Village).

3. Scope

Definition: The “impact and longevity” of the video—how long it will remain relevant and its potential reach.

Scale:

  • 4: High — Evergreen content with broad appeal, usable beyond initial context. Examples: website staples, teaching series (e.g., “God Owns It All”), long-term Jeff Norris content, LOL discipleship videos.
  • 3: Medium-High — Significant but shorter-term relevance, including seasonal projects (Holy Week, Easter, Christmas, Advent, Christmas Eve). Examples: campaign videos, multi-channel seasonal content.
  • 2: Medium — Event-driven or seasonal with moderate reach. Examples: summer series, event promos for specific ministries.
  • 1: Low — Short-term, limited reach. Examples: one-off event captures, single-use reels.

Complexity Score (1–12 Scale)

Definition: The level of resources, time, and effort required to complete the project. Complexity is rated separately and does not affect the priority score. It is a primary input to the capacity allocation formula. Video duration is a key factor in determining complexity.

Scale:

Rating Level Description Examples
1–2 Minimal Simple talking head, under 1 min, minimal edits, single take Quick announcement, single take with no effects
3–4 Low Basic graphics, 1–2 min, single shoot, simple B-roll Basic announcement, short intro video
5–6 Medium-Low Multiple takes, 2–4 min, moderate graphics, basic effects Standard teaching video, simple promo
7–8 Medium Multiple shoots, 4–8 min, moderate effects, professional B-roll, motion graphics, OR 30–45 min multi-camera live-switched productions Digging Deeper (live), testimonial with B-roll
9–10 High Complex edits, 8–15 min, multi-day production, advanced effects, narrative structure, OR 30–45 min multi-camera remote productions Digging Deeper (remote/Riverside), complex narrative
11–12 Very High Multi-location shoots, 15+ min, extensive post-production, motion graphics, complex workflows Feature-length content, complex motion graphics

Special Rules:

Digging Deeper (Live/In-Person): Complexity = 8

Digging Deeper (Remote/Riverside): Complexity = 9

All episodes in the same series use consistent complexity ratings

Capacity Allocation Formula

Once a project is scored, the system calculates how much of a team member’s weekly capacity the task will consume. This replaces the previous static “capacity score” system.

Formula:

Base = Complexity × 3.5 × Phase Multiplier
Priority Factor = 0.5 + (Priority Score ÷ 24)
Weekly Allocation % = (Base × Priority Factor) ÷ Duration in Weeks
Bounds: Minimum 5% Maximum 80%

Phase Multipliers

Production Phase Multiplier Rationale
Preproduction 0.8× Planning and scripting — lighter effort
Production 1.2× Filming and recording — moderate effort
Post Production 2.0× Editing and revisions — most intensive

B-Roll Multiplier

When B-roll capture is required, Production phase receives an additional 1.5× multiplier (1.2 × 1.5 = 1.8× effective).

Example Calculation

Example 1 — Complexity 7, Priority Score 10, 4-week duration, Production phase:

  • Base: 7 × 3.5 × 1.2 = 29.4
  • Priority Factor: 0.5 + (10 ÷ 24) = 0.917
  • Weekly Allocation: (29.4 × 0.917) ÷ 4 = 6.7% per week

Example 2 — Complexity 9, Priority Score 11, 2-week duration, Post Production phase:

  • Base: 9 × 3.5 × 2.0 = 63.0
  • Priority Factor: 0.5 + (11 ÷ 24) = 0.958
  • Weekly Allocation: (63.0 × 0.958) ÷ 2 = 30.2% per week

Manipulation Safeguards

The AI scoring system includes built-in protections:

  • Cross-verification: Self-reported form fields (Purpose, Audience, Scope selections) are treated as secondary. The AI analyzes the actual content summary and notes, overriding form selections when they conflict with the factual content.
  • Audit logging: All scoring overrides and mismatches are logged with reasoning for review.
  • Consistency checks: Series episodes (e.g., Digging Deeper) are enforced to maintain consistent complexity across all installments.

How to Use the System

  1. Submit a project in Asana with a clear name, notes, and content summary
  2. The system scores automatically — Priority, Complexity, Category, and Type are assigned within 15 minutes
  3. Review the dashboard — Check team capacity, upcoming deadlines, and at-risk projects at any time
  4. Plan with capacity in mind — For high-priority projects with high complexity, verify team members have sufficient capacity before scheduling
  5. Track delivery — Monitor on-time rates and allocation accuracy to continuously improve estimation
